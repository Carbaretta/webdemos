The Nitrogen Cycle: The Foundation of Aquarium Filtration

Before introducing fish to a newly set up aquarium, you must establish biological filtration through a process called the nitrogen cycle. Fish release toxic ammonia into the water through gills, urine, and decomposing solid waste. Leftover fish food also decays into ammonia. If ammonia levels rise, fish will experience gill damage, stress, and eventually death.

Biological filtration relies on colonies of beneficial, nitrifying bacteria. The cycle occurs in two stages: first, bacteria called Nitrosomonas colonize the aquarium filter and gravel, converting ammonia into nitrite. Nitrite is also highly toxic to fish. Next, a second group of bacteria (Nitrospira) develops to convert nitrite into nitrate. Nitrate is far less toxic and can safely accumulate in small amounts between scheduled water changes.

Establishing this biological filter usually takes 4 to 6 weeks. To start the cycle, add a source of ammonia (such as a tiny amount of fish food or a commercial bottled bacteria product) to the empty tank. Regularly test the water using a liquid test kit. You will see ammonia levels rise and fall, followed by a rise and fall in nitrites. The cycle is complete only when both ammonia and nitrite test at 0 parts per million (ppm), and nitrates begin to accumulate.

Understanding and Monitoring Essential Water Parameters

Maintaining stable water chemistry is critical for fish survival. Fish are cold-blooded organisms whose biological functions are directly affected by their aquatic environment. While different fish species are adapted to distinct natural habitats, the most important rule of water chemistry is avoiding rapid fluctuations, which stress fish and compromise their immune systems.

There are four core parameters you should monitor regularly:

  • pH (Potential Hydrogen): Measures how acidic or alkaline the water is on a scale of 0 to 14. A neutral pH is 7.0. Most community fish thrive between 6.5 and 7.5, but species like African cichlids prefer alkaline water (8.0+), while South American tetras prefer acidic conditions (6.0 - 6.8).
  • Temperature: Tropical fish require a stable temperature, typically between 24°C and 27°C (75°F to 80°F), maintained by an aquarium heater. Coldwater fish, like goldfish, thrive in cooler rooms without a heater (15°C to 20°C).
  • General Hardness (GH): Measures the concentration of dissolved calcium and magnesium ions. Hard water contains high levels of minerals, while soft water contains very few.
  • Carbonate Hardness (KH): Measures the water's buffering capacity. KH acts as a stabilizer that prevents sudden pH crashes, which can kill fish overnight. Maintain KH at 3° dKH or higher to keep your pH stable.

Always test your tap water before using it. Many municipal water supplies contain chlorine or chloramines to make it safe for humans, but these chemicals are lethal to fish. Always add a commercial tap water conditioner (dechlorinator) to neutralize these compounds before introducing new water to the tank.

Establishing Daily and Weekly Fish Maintenance Routines

A thriving aquarium requires simple, consistent maintenance. The most common cause of aquarium failure is overfeeding and lack of routine water changes. In the wild, fish constantly swim in massive volumes of fresh water that dilutes and carries away waste. In a closed home aquarium, waste products build up over time and must be removed manually.

Daily Care: Feed your fish once or twice a day. Only offer an amount of food they can completely consume within two minutes. Any food left sinking to the bottom will rot, polluting the water and causing ammonia spikes. Also, check that the filter is running smoothly, the heater indicator light is functioning, and all fish are swimming actively without gasping at the surface.

Weekly or Biweekly Care: Perform a 20% to 30% water change. Instead of scooping water from the surface, use a gravel vacuum (siphon) to clean the substrate. Push the vacuum cylinder into the gravel to lift out trapped debris, uneaten food, and fish waste. This removes solid pollutants and dilutes dissolved nitrates.

Filter Maintenance: Clean your filter media once a month to prevent clogging, which reduces water flow. Never rinse filter cartridges, sponges, or ceramic rings under the tap. Tap water contains chlorine that will instantly kill the beneficial biological bacteria colonizing the media. Instead, fill a bucket with siphoned tank water and gently squeeze or rinse the filter media in it to remove debris, preserving the life-saving bacteria.
